**Author:** User Researcher (Desk Research)
**Date:** 11 April 2026
**Purpose:** Inform the "Pick a starter pack" onboarding experience for basketch

## 1. Swiss Grocery Shopping Habits

### Key Findings

**Frequency and spend:**
- Swiss households spend approximately CHF 600-650/month on groceries (BFS Haushaltsbudgeterhebung). This is among the highest in Europe.
- Most households shop 2-3 times per week. The primary "big shop" is typically Thursday evening or Saturday morning (Sunday all stores closed).
- The average basket contains 15-25 items per trip.

**Where they shop:**
- Migros and Coop together hold ~70% of the Swiss grocery market (Migros ~35%, Coop ~30%, discounters ~15%, specialty ~20%).
- Most Swiss households are not loyal to one store -- they split between Migros and Coop depending on promotions, proximity, and product preferences. This is exactly the behavior basketch targets.
- Migros skews slightly more price-conscious; Coop skews slightly more premium/organic (Naturaplan, Karma lines). But both serve the full spectrum.
- Discounters Aldi and Lidl have grown from ~3% to ~15% market share since entering Switzerland (~2005/2009), primarily capturing the price-sensitive segment.

**Staple items -- the "Swiss standard basket":**
- Dairy is central: Milch, Butter, Käse, Joghurt, Rahm, Quark. Switzerland has the highest per-capita cheese consumption globally (~23 kg/year).
- Bread: Ruchbrot (most popular everyday bread), Zopf (Sunday tradition), Weggli, Toast.
- Meat: Poulet (chicken -- most consumed), Schweinefleisch (pork), Rindfleisch (beef), Cervelat (the Swiss national sausage), Bratwurst.
- Vegetables: Tomaten, Rüebli (carrots -- Swiss-German term), Gurken, Salat, Kartoffeln, Zwiebeln.
- Fruit: Bananen (#1 by volume), Äpfel (#2, very Swiss), Erdbeeren/Beeren (seasonal).
- Coffee: Switzerland is one of the world's top coffee consumers per capita (~8-9 kg/year). Nespresso-compatible capsules, filter coffee, and espresso beans all sell heavily.
- Chocolate: ~10 kg per capita/year. Lindt, Frey (Migros own), Cailler (Nestle/Coop stocked), branches.
- Convenience: Migros and Coop both have strong convenience/ready-meal lines (Anna's Best at Migros, Betty Bossi at Coop).

## 2. Swiss Dietary Patterns

### Home cooking vs. convenience

- Swiss residents eat at home for ~65-70% of meals (BFS data). This is lower than Southern Europe but higher than the UK.
- Lunch is historically the main warm meal (Mittagessen), though this is shifting toward evening meals as more people work outside the home.
- Ready meals (Fertiggerichte) and convenience products have grown ~4-5% annually. Migros "Anna's Best" and Coop "Betty Bossi" lines are market leaders.
- Meal kits (HelloFresh) have a Swiss presence but remain niche.

### Popular cuisine types (by frequency of home cooking)

1. **Swiss-German traditional:** Rösti, Zürcher Geschnetzeltes, Älplermagronen, Raclette, Fondue, Berner Platte. Seasonal/occasional rather than daily.
2. **Italian (dominant everyday cuisine):** Pasta, Risotto, Pizza, Minestrone. Italian food is essentially the default Swiss home-cooking style. ~40-50% of weeknight dinners.
3. **Pan-European comfort:** Schnitzel, Cordon Bleu, Pouletbrust with vegetables, salad plates, soups.
4. **Turkish/Balkan:** Kebab is the #1 takeaway food in Switzerland. At home: Köfte, Lahmacun ingredients, Ayran. Significant ~15% of population has Balkan/Turkish roots.
5. **Asian (growing):** Thai (pad thai, curry), Indian (curry, dal), Japanese (sushi rice, soy sauce). Mainly younger demographics (25-40).
6. **Latin American:** Still very niche in Switzerland.

### Meal patterns

- **Breakfast (Frühstück/Zmorge):** Bread + butter + jam (Konfitüre), Birchermüesli (Swiss invention), coffee, sometimes a Gipfeli (croissant).
- **Lunch (Mittagessen/Zmittag):** Increasingly a sandwich, salad, or warmed ready meal. Traditional: warm meal with meat, starch, vegetables.
- **Dinner (Abendessen/Znacht):** Often lighter: bread, cheese, cold cuts (Plättli/Apero-style). Younger households cook warm dinners.
- **Weekend:** Brunch culture is strong. Zopf + butter + jam on Sunday is near-universal.

## 3. Household Types in Switzerland

### Demographics (BFS 2024 data)

| Household type | % of households | Estimated # | Key grocery characteristics |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Single-person** | ~37% | ~1.4M | Smallest baskets, high convenience, price-sensitive on waste (smaller packs), more ready meals |
| **Couples without children** | ~28% | ~1.0M | Medium baskets, more cooking, wine/cheese/quality focus, dual income |
| **Families with children** | ~25% | ~0.9M | Largest baskets, highest spend, kids' products (Joghurt, Cerealien, Snacks), bulk buying |
| **Single parents** | ~5% | ~0.2M | Budget-conscious, convenience-dependent, family-sized staples |
| **Shared flats (WG)** | ~5% | ~0.2M (est.) | Student/young adult, highly price-sensitive, basics-heavy, international cooking |

### Grocery needs by household type

**Singles (37% of households -- largest segment):**
- Smaller pack sizes (250g meat, 500ml milk, 4-pack eggs)
- More ready meals and convenience products
- Higher proportion of non-food per trip
- Key items: Fertiggerichte, Salat, Brot, Joghurt, Kaffee, Bier/Wein

**Couples (28%):**
- Cook together on weekends, convenience on weeknights
- Quality-oriented: Bio/organic, specialty cheeses, wine
- Key items: Poulet, Gemüse (vegetables), Pasta, Olivenöl, Wein, Käse

**Families (25%):**
- Volume buying: 6-pack milk, 10-pack eggs, family-size cereal
- Kids' products: Joghurt (Fruchtzwerge etc.), Cerealien, Snacks, Säfte
- Balanced nutrition pressure from parents
- Key items: Milch, Brot, Eier, Poulet, Reis, Pasta, Rüebli, Joghurt, Müesli, Obst

**Students/WG (~5%):**
- Absolute lowest basket value
- Heavy on basics: Pasta, Reis, Brot, Eier, Tomaten
- Very price-sensitive -- actively chase Aktionen
- Key items: Pasta, Reis, Brot, Eier, Zwiebeln, Tomaten, Bier, Chips

## 4. Dietary Trends in Switzerland

### Vegetarian/Vegan

- ~5% of Swiss residents identify as vegetarian, ~1-2% as vegan (Swissveg, 2024).
- ~20-25% identify as "flexitarian" (reduce meat but not eliminate).
- Both Migros (V-Love line) and Coop (Karma line) have launched extensive plant-based ranges since ~2019.
- Plant-based meat alternatives (Beyond Meat, V-Love, Planted -- a Swiss startup) are available in both stores.
- Swiss per-capita meat consumption has declined ~10% over the past decade (from ~52 kg to ~47 kg/year).

### Organic/Bio

- Switzerland has one of the highest organic market shares in Europe: ~11-12% of total food retail (Bio Suisse, FiBL).
- Migros Bio (green label), Coop Naturaplan (most recognized organic brand in Switzerland), Demeter products.
- Bio premiums are typically 20-40% over conventional.
- Bio is more concentrated among: higher income (>CHF 9K/month household), age 30-50, urban, families with young children.

### Other dietary segments

| Segment | Est. % of shoppers | Key products in Swiss retail |
|---|---|---|
| **Flexitarian** | 20-25% | Quorn, V-Love (Migros), Karma (Coop), Planted, Tofu, Falafel |
| **Lactose-free** | 8-10% (intolerance) | "Laktosefrei" labeled dairy: Milch, Joghurt, Käse, Rahm (Migros "free from", Coop "free from") |
| **Gluten-free** | 3-5% (purchase regularly) | "Glutenfrei" Brot, Pasta, Mehl, Müesli (both stores have dedicated sections) |
| **Protein-focused/Fitness** | 5-8% (growing) | Migros "High Protein" range, Coop "Karma Protein", Quark, Pouletbrust, Eier |
| **Sugar-reduced** | Growing | "Weniger Zucker" labeled products, Zero drinks |

### Relevance to starter packs

The flexitarian/plant-based segment is large enough (20-25%) to warrant a dedicated starter pack. Lactose-free and gluten-free are too narrow for standalone packs but could be mentioned as customization options.

## 5. Cultural Grocery Patterns

### Expat vs. Swiss-born shopping

Switzerland has ~25% foreign-born residents (BFS). The largest groups:

| Origin | % of foreign residents | Grocery impact |
|---|---|---|
| **German** | ~15% | Similar habits to Swiss-German, slight preference for specific brands |
| **Italian** | ~15% | Strong Italian cooking tradition, higher pasta/olive oil/tomato consumption |
| **Portuguese** | ~12% | Bacalhau, Pasteis de Nata (niche), otherwise adapted |
| **Turkish/Balkan (Kosovo, Serbia, Bosnia, North Macedonia)** | ~15% | Halal meat, flatbreads, yogurt-heavy, specific spices, bulk rice/lentils |
| **Indian subcontinent** | ~3-4% | Basmati rice, dal, spices, ghee, paneer (limited at Migros/Coop), chutneys |
| **East/Southeast Asian** | ~3-4% | Soy sauce, tofu, noodles, rice, coconut milk, sriracha |
| **Other European (French, Spanish)** | ~15% | Close to Swiss-French/Mediterranean patterns |

**Key insight for basketch:** The "Indian Kitchen" pack already serves the Indian expat community well. The Turkish/Balkan community is larger (~15% of foreigners) but has no starter pack. Italian cooking is so mainstream it is essentially covered by Mediterranean + Swiss Basics. The Asian segment is growing fast.

### Migros vs. Coop customer demographics

- **Migros:** Historically positioned as "the people's store." Stronger with families, budget-conscious shoppers. M-Budget line is iconic. No alcohol sales (except Migrolino/Denner subsidiary). Stronger in German-speaking Switzerland.
- **Coop:** Positioned as slightly more premium. Stronger organic (Naturaplan). Sells alcohol. Coop City/Karma for premium/trendy. Stronger presence in French-speaking Switzerland.
- **Both serve everyone** -- the differences are at the margin, not fundamental.

### Popular international ingredients available at Migros/Coop

Both stores have "World Foods" / "Asia" / "Mexiko" aisles with:
- Sojasauce, Sriracha, Kokosmilch, Reisnudeln, Curry-Paste, Basmati-Reis
- Tortillas, Salsa, Taco-Gewürz
- Falafel-Mischung, Hummus, Tahini
- Naan, Papadums (limited)

## 6. Competitive Analysis: Onboarding Patterns

### How competitors organize categories/starter experiences

**Rappn.ch:**
- No starter pack concept. User arrives at a search bar + category browse.
- Categories: Getränke, Frisch, Tiefkühler, Nahrungsmittel, Haushalt, Pflege, Süsswaren.
- Onboarding: none. Immediate deal browsing.

**Bring! (shopping list app):**
- New users see an empty list. No templates or starter packs.
- Items are added by typing (autocomplete from their database).
- "Frequently added" items surface after a few uses.
- Categories are store-aisle oriented: Milchprodukte, Gemüse, Obst, Fleisch, Getränke, Haushalt, Tiefkühl.
- **Missed opportunity** that basketch can exploit: Bring! has no "quick start" templates.

**Too Good To Go:**
- Not relevant for starter pack design -- users browse surprise bags by store/location.

**Profital:**
- Digital flyer browsing. No personalization onboarding.
- Category: by retailer, then by flyer (e.g., "Migros KW15 Aktionis").

**Smood / Farmy (Swiss online grocery):**
- Smood: Recipe-based suggestion "What would you like to cook?" approach.
- Farmy: Curated boxes ("Bio Gemüse Box", "Früchte Box", "Studentenbox").
- **Relevant insight:** Farmy's "boxes" concept is the closest to starter packs. Their categories: Familien, Singles, Sportler, Veganer.

**International comparison -- Trolley (UK):**
- Trolley does cross-store price comparison (Tesco, Sainsbury's, Asda).
- Onboarding: "Popular items" auto-populated + search to add.
- No persona-based templates.

### Gap analysis

No Swiss grocery app offers persona-based starter templates. The closest is Farmy's box concept but it is for delivery, not for deal tracking. This is a genuine differentiation for basketch.

## 7. Price Sensitivity Segments

### Who is most price-sensitive?

| Segment | Price sensitivity | Behavior | Estimated size |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Students** | Very high | Chase every Aktion, buy M-Budget/Prix Garantie, shop at Aldi/Lidl, minimal basket | ~200K |
| **Young singles (20-30)** | High | Budget-conscious but will splurge on experiences (wine, eating out) | ~400K |
| **Young families (first child)** | High | Expenses spike with baby, suddenly very aware of grocery costs | ~200K |
| **Retirees (AHV income only)** | High | Fixed income, highly routine shopping, very loyal to promotions | ~300K |
| **Working couples (30-50, no kids)** | Medium | Dual income, will buy Bio/premium, less price-sensitive | ~500K |
| **Upper middle class families** | Low | Buy quality/Bio regardless, convenience over savings | ~300K |

**Key insight for basketch:** The primary deal-comparison audience is students, young singles/couples, and young families. Retirees are price-sensitive but less likely to use a web app. Working couples without kids are a secondary audience -- they use basketch for convenience ("save me time"), not desperation.

### Price sensitivity by category

- **Fresh produce:** Low sensitivity (quality matters, people pay more for freshness)
- **Meat:** Medium-high sensitivity (significant price differences between stores, 20-40% discount deals are common)
- **Dairy:** Medium sensitivity (habitual buying, but will switch for 30%+ off)
- **Non-food/household:** Very high sensitivity (no perceived quality difference, pure price competition -- perfect for deal comparison)
- **Chocolate/snacks:** Medium (impulse buys, but 50% off Lindt will move people)
- **Coffee:** High (regular purchase, significant per-unit savings at scale)

## 10. Implementation Notes

### Items per pack
- Keep 15-16 items per pack. This matches the "15-20 regular items" finding from shopping habit research.
- Users should be able to add/remove items after picking a pack (this is already in the design).

### Keyword quality
- All keywords above are German (matching Migros/Coop product naming).
- Each pack should use the same `excludeTerms` and `preferTerms` patterns already established in the current seed data.
- New keywords that need exclude/prefer terms: fladenbrot, hafermilch, avocado, erdnussbutter, cottage cheese, fischstäbchen, windeln, cervelat, basilikum, zitronen, pelati, tiefkühlpizza, haferflocken, süsskartoffeln.

### Pack naming convention
- Display labels in English (consistent with current English UI).
- Except "Studentenküche", "Familientisch", "Pflanzlich" which use German names as they are more culturally resonant and unique. Alternative: translate all to English if consistency is preferred.
- Machine names (slugs) in kebab-case as per existing convention.

### What to track
- Which pack users select most (to validate persona sizing).
- Drop-off rate per pack (do users customize heavily, suggesting the pack did not match well?).
- Items most frequently added/removed after pack selection (informs future pack refinement).

## 11. Open Questions for Product Decision

1. **Should pack labels be English or German?** Current: English. Recommendation: English (matches UI language), but German names like "Studentenküche" and "Familientisch" have stronger cultural identity. User decision.

2. **Should we allow multi-select?** E.g., a vegetarian family might want "Pflanzlich" + "Familientisch" merged. Current design appears single-select + customize. This is probably fine for MVP.

3. **Is 8 packs too many choices?** Choice paralysis risk. Could launch with 6 (drop Fitness & Balkan) and add them in v2 based on demand. User decision.

4. **Should the "General Mix" pack be removed entirely or renamed?** Recommendation: replace with "Studentenküche" since General Mix overlaps too heavily with Swiss Basics and has no persona identity.
